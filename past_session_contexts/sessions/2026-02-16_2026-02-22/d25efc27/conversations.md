## Turn 6
### Query
What are the tests for gluten disease in children

### Answer
In children, testing for “gluten disease” (celiac disease) usually includes blood tests, sometimes genetic tests, and often an intestinal biopsy, depending on guidelines and results.   
IgA anti–tissue transglutaminase (tTG‑IgA): main screening test for most ages.   
Total serum IgA: done with tTG‑IgA to detect IgA deficiency (otherwise tTG‑IgA can be falsely negative).   
If IgA deficient or child is very young (<2 years), IgG‑based tests are added: tTG‑IgG and/or deamidated gliadin peptide (DGP‑IgG, sometimes DGP‑IgA).   
Small‑intestinal (duodenal) biopsy by endoscopy: classic “gold standard” to confirm villous atrophy in most guidelines, especially in North America.   
In some pediatric guidelines, a diagnosis can be made *without* biopsy if tTG‑IgA is ≥10× upper limit of normal and endomysial antibody (EMA‑IgA) is also positive, in a child with typical symptoms.   
HLA‑DQ2 / HLA‑DQ8 typing: tests for genetic susceptibility; if both are absent, celiac disease is very unlikely.   
Other labs sometimes done at diagnosis: iron studies, folate, vitamin D, thyroid function, and growth parameters, to look for complications or associated conditions.   

If you tell me the child’s age and whether they already started a gluten‑free diet, I can suggest exactly which tests to ask the doctor for and in what order.
